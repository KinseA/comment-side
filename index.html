A look back over how we got here and why

As recently as 21 years ago being gay in Ireland was illegal. There were calls for homosexual acts to be decriminalised for years but the road to actual legislation began in the courts in 1977. Senator David Norris took a case to the High Court which was rejected in 1980. It was then rejected by the Supreme Court in 1983 before Norris took his case to the European Court of Human Rights. The European judges ruled that the Irish laws contravened the Convention on Human Rights. Five years later those laws were changed.

Decriminalisation preceded a series of other social reforms in Ireland, including the Divorce Referendum. The 1990s was a time of huge social change. Many believe that the introduction of the 1996 Family Law (Divorce) Act was a defining moment which demonstrated how the Irish people were ready to put some distance between the Catholic Church and the state.

During the build up to this referendum the levels of support for another social change have changed drastically, even in the short time since the Divorce Referendum was held. Most polls building up to the gay marriage vote have indicated that it will comfortably pass. An Ipsos/MRBI poll for The Irish Times in December suggested that 71% of people would vote in favour of gay marriage. While a Red C poll in January this year said 76% will vote yes. In comparison the divorce referendum passed by just over nine thousand votes - failing in most rural areas. Dublin has always had more liberal voting patterns, however now people in the countryside seem to be embracing change more readily. The Red C poll from January indicated 84% of people in Dublin were in favour of gay marriage, but also showed that 80% of those in Connacht and Ulster would vote yes.

From decriminalisation to a referendum on marriage equality, many things have changed over the past 21 years. In 2010 the Civil Partnership and Certain Rights and Obligations of Cohabitants Act was passed. It made it possible for those in same sex relationships to enter into civil partnerships. It was a game changer, with the first ceremonies taking place in the Spring of 2011. By 2012 a civil partnership had taken place in every county in Ireland. However some viewed the act as simply a stepping stone towards full marriage equality. Various LGBT groups state that there is still work to be done to reach their ultimate goal of equality for same sex couples and their families.

As was the case in the Divorce Referendum changing social attitudes played a significant role in bringing the Marriage Referendum about. Interestingly a 2008 Millward Brown poll revealed that 81% of people agreed that everyone in Ireland should receive equal treatment from the state, regardless of their sexual orientation. Yet it took a further seven years before it would come before the people for a vote.
